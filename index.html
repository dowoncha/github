What is AI?

Types of AI:

  • Artificial Narrow Intelligence (Weak AI) = An artificial intelligence system that can only specialize in one area and thats it.
  • Artificial General Intelligence (AGI/Strong AI) = Intelligence of a machine that could successfully perform any intellectual task that a human being can and is as smart as a human across the board.
  • Artificial Superintelligence (ASI) = Said by Oxford philosopher Nick Bostrom to be "an intellect that is much smarter than the best human brains in practically every field, including scientific creativity, general wisdom, and social skills."

Want to know more? Then check out this article from the Huffington Post

Requirements of AI:

  1. Reason
  2. Represent Knowledge
  3. Plan
  4. Learn
  5. Communicate in Natural Language
  6. Integrate Skills Towards Common Goals

How do we know its the real thing?

By far the most well known is the Turing Test

Next is a test that is considered to (probably) be more difficult is the Coffee Test

Our next way of testing AI which is believed to be a more challenging definition is the Robot College Student Test

The final test for AI and considered to be "unfair" in difficulty because not all humans can complete it is the Employment Test

THE TURING TEST

Man vs. Computer: One participant must be able to distinguish human from computer. If the participant cannot distinguish the two then the computer has passed.

THE COFFEE TEST

A test created by Ben Goertzel and made more popular behind the voice of Steve Wozniak where the AI passes if it can go into an average American house and figure out how to make coffee, including identifying the coffee machine, figuring out what the buttons do, finding the coffee in the cabinet, etc. on its own

THE ROBOT COLLEGE STUDENT TEST

This test was also created in 2012 by Ben Goertzel and suggests that we will have created an artificial general intelligence once a robot can enroll in a human university and take classes in the same way as humans, and get its degree.

THE EMPLOYMENT TEST

Nils John Nilsson (an american born computer scientist and one AI's founding researchers) suggested the most difficult test for AI where it should be able to be employed, meaning the AI must have at least the poritential to completely automate economically important jobs.

HISTORY OF AI

1931 - Kurt Godel

The work of Kurt Godel left a foundation for other AI pioneers to build upon. Godel is credited with setting up theoretical computer science. TCS includes topics such as data structures, algorithms, machine learning, and more. These fields have become extremely important to AI as we see it today.

1936 - Alan Turing

Alan Turing is most famous for cracking the German Enigma machine during World War II, but perhaps his largest success was the invention of the Turing machine in 1936. Turing was able to reformulate much of the work that Godel had started and did so much more efficiently.

1935-1941 - Konrad Zuse

Meanwhile Konrad Zuse was busy working on his own additions to AI. He built the very first functioning program-controlled computers between 1935 and 1941. He even went on to write the first chess playing program which was a basic AI in itself.

1940s - Hardware Advancements

Now, thanks to Godel, Turing, and Zuse, the beginnings of modern computer science were in place. Through the 1940s the technology of the computers themselves were drastically improved. This improvement was critical to the success of future AI attempts.

1950s - AI Defined

In 1950 Alan Turing created a test that determined whether a machine was intelligent or not. Shortly thereafter in 1956 John McCarthy coined the term Artificial Intelligence.

Celebrating 75 years of AI - History and Outlook: the Next 25 Years: https://arxiv.org/abs/0708.4311

1960s - Probability Based AI

Ray Solomonoff combined theorectical computer science and probability theory which would help pave the way for optimal prediction based on experience. Today this has become machine learning.

1960s - AI and Biology

Also in the 60s Ingo Rechenberg developed a form of artificial evolution. This helped optimize complex tasks like creating perfect aerodynamic airplane wings.

1995 - Driving AI

The 1990s AI advancement was driven primarily by rapidly improving hardware. In 1995 Ernst Dickmans and Mercedes-Benz built a robotic car that drove 1000 miles by itself.
